Musical Instruments

Benguela

In terms of the musical structure, Benguela is played in a 6/8 time signature and has a distinct melody that is repeated throughout the rhythm. The berimbau, a one-stringed musical instrument, typically plays the main melody while the atabaque, a type of drum, provides the rhythm and the pandeiro, a type of tambourine, adds accents and fills.

Benguela is also associated with certain movements and techniques in Capoeira Angola. For example, the ginga, which is the basic movement in Capoeira, is often done in a slow and deliberate manner during Benguela. Additionally, kicks such as meia lua de frente and tesoura are commonly used during this rhythm.

In terms of the meaning behind Benguela, it is believed to have originated from the region of Benguela in Angola, which was a major center for slave trade during the colonial period. The rhythm is said to represent the struggle and resistance of enslaved Africans who were brought to Brazil and used Capoeira as a form of self-defense and rebellion against their oppressors.
